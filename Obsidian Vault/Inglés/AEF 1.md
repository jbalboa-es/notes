# 4A Whose?; possessive 's
- Use 's with a person to talk about family and possessions
- We use s', not 's, with regular plural nouns
- With irregular plural nouns, use 's
- Whose...? to ask about possessions
	- Whose is this bag? It's Maria's bag
	- Whose bag is this? It's Maria's
- Don't use a thing + 's, prefer 'of the thing' or only put as an adjective
- Be careful with Who's and Whose, are pronounced the same.
# 4B preposition: at, in, on to
- in for parts of the day and seasons. Also with months, years and centuries . is for enclosed spaces, larger areas or periods.
- on for days and *the weekend*. Also with dates. is for surfaces or things that are touching something else, or specific days.
- at for times of the day and *night*. is for specific points or times. 
- to for movement or direction.  don't use before home
- in and at for position:
	- at + work, home, school
	- in + other places
	- can use in or at with some public places: restaurant, movies, etc. 

*at the end* to specific point in time.
*in the end* to outcome or conclusion.

When you don't have to use the in, on, and at prepositions at all:
- before general time expressions
- before specific verbs or phrases that don't require prepositions
	- *the room*
- before *next* or *last*
# 4C adverbs and expressions of frequency 
- adverbs of frequency go before all main verbs, except verb to be, in this case go after.
- use + verb with *never* and *hardly ever*
- in negative sentences adverb goes between don't/doesn´t and the verb
- expressions of frequency usually go at the end of a sentence or verb phrase
# 5A can/can't
- has different meanings
	- I know how to
	- It's possible for me
	- It's OK/It´s permitted
- can you...?
	- Please do it
- can I...?
	-  Is it OK if I do it?
- don't use *to* after *can*.
# 5B present continuous: be + verb + ing
- for things that are happening now
- also we use that with longer periods of time like *today* or *this week*
- spelling rules:
	- add -ing
	- delete e and put -ing
	- one vowel + one consonant = double consonant + -ing
# 5C simple present or present continuous?
- simple present to say what we usually do or things that are normally true. often with adverbs of frequency.
- present continuous to say what is happening now and often with *right now*, *today* and *this week*
